India is caught in a flux between unbelievable growth on one hand, and widening inequalities on the other. To navigate this wide spectrum of people, and to form inclusive policies such that it caters to every member of a very diverse society, remains a challenge to the providers of care.

Following a decade of rapid growth fuelled by a prosperous service sector, indicators of health are still poor, healthcare infrastructure insufficient and plagued by inefficiency, and personal expenditure on health, which many can ill-afford, continues to be high.

Over the four days of the Symposium, the participants will be addressed by mentors from the fields of technology, policy and the social sciences, and will debate positions to improve present conditions.

 

Health and Technology

The Indian medical technology industry is expected to grow from US$2.7 billion in 2008 to US$14 billion in 2020. Although this is an age of remarkable advances in healthcare, this has not resulted into its transformation into an inclusive and accessible provision. Most health facilities across the country face staggering infrastructure issues of the most basic sort.

Under this category, topics such as the implications of big data, democratisation of healthcare through access to available new technologies and cost-effective, safe and affordable technological innovations will be explored. Big data in healthcare is an area of tremendous potential, and has implications across several areas- in the prediction of diseases, comparison of symptoms and test results to form large databases, and even helps us develop monitoring services with smartphone applications. Under this, risks involved with privacy, security and management will also be discussed.

Such issues are of paramount importance in recent times. Join our industrial experts at the Symposium in discussing this and more.

 

Health and Policy

Although healthcare is a $65 billion industry, India’s investment in healthcare (about 2% of the GDP) is astonishingly disproportionate to the population of the country. Apart from the considerable infrastructure deficit (in medical colleges, doctors, staff and hospitals), about 85% of the country is not covered by an insurance scheme, and most have no access to even primary healthcare facilities, as it still remains a largely private-sector enterprise which only the wealthy can access.

 

Under policy, the students and mentors will also engage in discussions related to the emerging IPR debate on India’s standards of patent flexibility. The legal aspects surrounding the health sector in India are of paramount importance as it has contributed to the development of the unique Indian pharmaceutical industry. The patent laws placing affordability at the centre rather than patent production was instrumental in a flourishing domestic industry. IPR raises important questions of equal access and ownership of knowledge, and to what extent each can be justified.

The track mentors will also discuss the problems of implementing health insurance and FDI in healthcare. Through this exercise, we hope to explore novel avenues within the ambit of policy formation to improve current conditions.

 

As a country of great diversity, India encounters unique challenges because of several social factors which determine access to well-devised healthcare. These include an unequal distribution of wealth, the powerful forces caste and gender, and also geography.
